# Request-to-Maintain Process Analysis (BPM Project - Ford Service Center)

## Overview
This project analyzes the **Request-to-Maintain process** at a Ford Service Center, which manages customer vehicle maintenance requests from submission to completion or cancellation.

The project applies Business Process Management (BPM) techniques to model, analyze, and redesign the process in order to improve efficiency, reduce delays, and enhance service quality.

---

## Process Scope
The process includes:
- Warranty validation  
- Initial inspection  
- Quotation preparation  
- Parts availability check  
- Service booking  
- Payment handling  
- Maintenance execution or cancellation  

---

## Stakeholders
- Customer  
- Service Coordination Department  
- Warranty Department  
- Warehouse Department  
- External Garage  
- Certified Spare Parts Suppliers  

---

## Key Analyses

### 1. Value-Added Analysis
Activities were classified into:
- Value-Added (VA)
- Business Value-Added (BVA)
- Non-Value-Added (NVA)

### 2. Waste Analysis
Identified inefficiencies include:
- Long waiting times
- Manual communication between departments
- Over-processing steps
- Inventory and scheduling delays

### 3. Bottleneck Analysis
Major bottlenecks were found in:
- Customer response delays (3–5 days waiting time)
- Quotation approval process
- Payment handling stage (7 days cycle time)

### 4. Quantitative Analysis
- Overall Cycle Time: 15.9 days  
- Processing Time: 13.15 hours  
- Cycle Time Efficiency (CTE): 10.3%  
- Resource Utilization: 46%  
- Annual Demand: 500 requests  
- System Capacity: 1080 requests/year  

---

## Redesign (TO-BE Improvements)
The process was improved using BPM redesign techniques:
- Automation of warranty verification and request registration  
- Parallel execution of parts availability and quotation preparation  
- Merging service booking tasks into a single activity  
- Automated invoicing and payment tracking with system timers  

---

## Key Findings
- The process is heavily affected by waiting times and manual steps  
- Most delays come from customer-dependent decisions  
- Resource capacity is sufficient but underutilized  
- Major efficiency loss is due to non-value-added waiting activities
